Comparison of chlamydia to other similar conditions: UTI, gonorrhea, and BV

In this chapter, you will find a comparison of chlamydia to other common medical conditions. It will help you understand the most important differences from a doctor’s standpoint.


Often, different conditions have similar symptoms and signs. Any experienced doctor keeps in mind other conditions that look and present similarly. It is called a differential diagnosis.

The differences between similar conditions can be very subtle. So, all signs and symptoms should be analyzed regarding these (and other) factors:

  • timing of the symptoms
  • how the symptoms started
  • how the symptoms have evolved over time
  • statistics for the suspected condition in the geographic area

In this section, we will help you to distinguish a chlamydia infection from other similar conditions such as UTI, gonorrhea, and BV.


We have practical information side by side in tables so it will be easier to see the similarities and differences between those conditions and chlamydia.


The tables will be followed by frequently asked questions from real patients and an expert doctor’s answers to them.


Let’s proceed!

Chlamydia vs. urinary tract infection

Here you will learn how a doctor tells the difference between chlamydia and urinary tract infection (UTI).


UTI is inflammation of the urethra (urethritis) or bladder (cystitis) that can lead to a kidney infection (pyelonephritis) if left untreated.


Signs and symptoms such as frequent urination, a burning sensation while urinating, urgent urination, and lower abdominal pain—commonly referred as UTI symptoms—can result from either UTI or chlamydia.


When differentiating between two conditions, it’s important to analyze sign and symptoms in the right context. The right context in this case means considering these things:

  • gender (UTI is more common in females)
  • what preceded the signs and symptoms (new sexual partner?)
  • when symptoms appeared first
  • how the disease has been evolving

It’s important to distinguish between these two conditions because chlamydia, unlike UTI (which can get better without treatment), can cause irreversible reproductive system damage (mainly in females) if it’s not detected and treated.

Frequently Asked Questions

Here we summarize real patients’ questions, grouped by topic, and Dr. Fuzayloff’s answers to them. “Dr. F” (his patients’ name for him) has been an STD doctor for over two decades in Midtown Manhattan, NYC.

  • I have a genital discharge. Is it UTI or chlamydia?

    With the information we have available in your question, it’s most likely chlamydia. Discharge is very uncommon with UTIs. But remember that UTI and chlamydia are diagnosed at the same time in 20% of females, so a discharge doesn’t rule out a UTI.

    To answer the question more definitively, we would like to have more information:

    Gender

    • UTI is considered first in females because it’s very common
    • An STD needs to be ruled out first in males

    Age

    • The younger you are, the greater the chance of chlamydia

    Sexual history

    • A new partner or unprotected contact increases the likelihood it’s chlamydia

    How soon after exposure symptoms developed

    • Chlamydia has a longer incubation period: 3 weeks on average
    • UTI usually happens the next day

    How symptoms have evolved over time

    Discharge characteristics: color, consistency, amount

    What I’m trying to say here is that doctors put a lot of small pieces of a history and examination together to decide if one diagnosis is more likely than another.

  • I am a 25-year-old male and I have burning sensation when I urinate. Which is more likely to be the cause, UTI or chlamydia? Why?

    It’s not possible to answer the question based on the information provided. The only thing that can be said is that chlamydia is more likely for the male gender and for this age group statistically.


    A few things need to be clarified to help us make a more accurate assumption:

    Sexual history

    • Any new partner recently?

      • If so, that increases the likelihood of chlamydia

    • Was the contact unprotected (including receiving oral sex)?

      • If so, chlamydia becomes more likely

    Symptoms

    • Do you ha ve any other symptoms besides a burning sensation? Any discharge? Staining of the underwear?

      • If so, chlamydia is more likely because a discharge is rare with UTI

    • Are the burning symptoms logical and predictable?

      • Both UTI and chlamydia cause more intense symptoms with urination when the inflamed area is irritated by passing urine. They rarely give you a persistent feeling of pain or pain at a specific time of day (before going to bed, for example). If the symptoms are not logical and predictable, it’s possible that the symptoms are psychological. But testing should be done before making that assumption.

    Note: All of these things help in deciding what the likely diagnosis is, but they don’t establish the diagnosis. Testing is always necessary.

  • I am a female and I have a burning sensation when I urinate. Can I safely assume I have a UTI?

    Most likely that’s a correct assumption. UTI is a common condition for females. Almost 60% of females have been diagnosed with UTI at some point in their life. UTI is 14 times more common in females than in males.

    Why UTIs are common in females:

    Anatomic differences:

    • Women have a shorter urethra (the tube that excretes urine), making it easier for bacteria to enter the upper parts of the urinary tract.
    • The lower third of the urethra is continuously exposed to vaginal and anal bacteria because the vaginal, anal, and urethral openings are located near each other.
    • Infectious agents from the penis are easily spread to the urethra during sexual intercourse.

    Residual urine:

    • Unlike men, females don’t empty their bladder completely when they urinate. The residual urine creates optimal conditions for infections and contributes to the development of the disease.

    However, even though we know that URI is common in females, we still need more information to make an accurate assumption.

    Sex history

    • Any new partner recently? Unprotected sex (including receiving oral)?
      • If so, chlamydia and other STDs need to be ruled out.

    Additional symptoms

    • Any other symptoms, such as discharge, bleeding with intercourse, or bleeding between periods?
      • If so, the likelihood of an STD.
