Females: chlamydia vs. bacterial vaginosis

Here we compare chlamydia and bacterial vaginosis (BV). BV, unlike chlamydia, is not considered a sexually transmitted disease. It affects only females and can be induced by sex but not transmitted by a sex partner.


Sexual activity can change the pH of the vagina and induce BV symptoms. Other common things that can lead to BV are low immunity and pregnancy. BV is linked to an imbalance of “good” and “harmful” bacteria normally found in a the vagina. BV can increase the chance of getting an STD.

Signs and Symptoms: differences and similarities

Similarities Differences
  • Pain with urination
  • Pain during sex

Discharge color

  • BV causes a thin, whitish discharge, whereas chlamydia causes a transparent, scanty discharge

Discharge smell

  • BV discharge has a specific fishy smell

Vaginal symptoms

  • BV causes significant vaginal itch, while chlamydia rarely causes itch

Frequently Asked Questions

Here we summarize real patients’ questions, grouped by topic, and Dr. Fuzayloff’s answers to them. “Dr. F” (his patients’ name for him) has been an STD doctor for over two decades in Midtown Manhattan, NYC.

  • I am a 25-year-old female and I have itching and burning around the vaginal opening and a fishy odor: Is it BV or chlamydia?

    It’s challenging to establish a diagnosis based on the signs and symptoms alone. However, based on the above information, it’s more likely that you have BV.


    Let’s see what suggests BV over chlamydia in this case.

    More vaginal symptoms with BV

    Unlike chlamydia (which affects the cervix, which is deeper in the body), BV affects the outer portion of the vagina and for that reason has more obvious vaginitis symptoms of itch around the vagina opening, redness, and burning. Cervicitis (chlamydia) causes fewer vaginal symptoms.

    Fishy smell

    Any vaginal discharge can be smelly, but certain conditions are well known to have a specific smell. BV is known to have a fishy-smelling discharge. This odor is the result of the altered pH level in the vagina caused by the interaction between healthy vaginal bacteria and unhealthy bacteria.


    Other infections that affect the vagina and cause inflammation are trichomoniasis and yeast infection. They need to be ruled out too.


    The below table summarizes the most important differences that help a doctor make an assumption about whether a patient has BV or chlamydia.

    BV Chlamydia
    Lower abdominal pain
    A “fishy” odor from the vagina (especially after sexual intercourse)
    Bleeding between periods
    Pain during sex
    Bleeding during sex
    Scanty, clear discharge
    Thin, whitish discharge
    New partner or unprotected sex
    Itching around the vaginal opening
