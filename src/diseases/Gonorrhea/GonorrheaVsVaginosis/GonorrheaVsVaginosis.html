Females only: gonorrhea versus bacterial vaginosis (BV)

Bacterial vaginosis , unlike gonorrhea, is not considered a sexually transmitted disease. It affects only females and can be induced by sex but not transmitted by a sexual partner. Sexual activity can change the pH of the vagina and induce BV symptoms.


Other common things that can lead to BV are low immunity and pregnancy. BV is linked to an imbalance of “good” and “harmful” bacteria that are normally found in the vagina. BV can increase the chances of getting an STD.

Gonorrhea versus BV: similarities and differences

Similarities Differences
  • Pain with urination
  • Pain during sex

Discharge color

  • BV: thin, whitish discharge Gonorrhea: thick, abundant, yellowish discharge

Discharge smell

  • BV discharge has a specific fishy smell

Vaginal symptoms

  • BV causes significant vaginal itch
  • Gonorrhea rarely causes itch

Frequently Asked Questions

  • I am a 25-year-old female and I have itching and burning around the vaginal opening and a “fishy” odor. Is it BV or gonorrhea?

    It’s challenging to establish a diagnosis based on the signs and symptoms alone. However, with this information, you’re more likely experiencing BV. Let’s see what’s suggesting BV over gonorrhea.

    More vaginal symptoms with BV

    Unlike gonorrhea (which affects the cervix, which is deeper in the body), BV affects the outer portion of vagina. For that reason, BV creates more obvious vaginitis symptoms of itch around the vaginal opening, redness, and burning. Cervicitis (gonorrhea) causes fewer vaginal symptoms.

    Fishy smell

    Any vaginal discharge can be smelly, but certain conditions are well known for a specific smell. BV is well known for a fishy smelling discharge. This odor is the result of an altered pH level in the vagina caused by the interaction between healthy vaginal bacteria and “unhealthy” bacteria.


    Other infections that affect the vagina and cause inflammation are trichomoniasis and yeast infection. They need to be ruled out too.


    The table below summarizes the most important differences that helps a doctor decide which condition the patient is more likely to have, gonorrhea or BV.

    BV Gonorrhea
    Lower abdominal pain
    A “fishy” odor from the vagina (especially after sexual intercourse)
    Bleeding between periods
    Pain during sex
    Bleeding during sex
    Scanty, clear discharge
    Thin, whitish discharge
    New partner or unprotected sex
    Itching around the vaginal opening
