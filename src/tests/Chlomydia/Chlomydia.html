AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Chlamydia testing is recommended for both symptomatic and asymptomatic people.

NO symptoms

  • Sexually active female 25 and younger
  • People with multiple sex partners
  • People who have had unprotected sex with a new partner
  • People who have a chlamydia-positive partner
  • Sexually active homosexual male

WITH symptoms

  • Sexually active people who have chlamydia-related signs and symptoms
  • Can be tested at any time, irrespective of the time that has passed since the exposure.

WHEN TO GET TESTED?

Here we will summarize what is known about timing (how soon the test can be done) and the accuracy of chlamydia testsin various anatomic areas for asymptomatic people.

If you have symptoms, the test can be done any time, irrespective of the amount of time that has passed since the sexual encounter that you believe may have given you chlamydia.

Genital swab 2-7 days after exposure 98.3% *
Rectal swab 2-7 days after exposure 94.7% *
Oral swab 2-7 days after exposure 80% *
Urine test 2-7 days after exposure 94.3% *

*Accuracy, if is done 2 weeks after exposure

TEST TYPES

Unlike other infections, chlamydia, if contracted, stays at the point of initial contact, so a negative test of one area of the body doesn’t rule out the possibility of having chlamydia in another area. This means that all “susceptible” areas that came into contact with the partner’s potentially affected areas during sex should be tested.


  • Male: Urine and urethral swab
  • Female: Urine and urethral swab
  • Anal receiving sex: Anal swab
  • Giving oral sex: Throat swab

TEST PREPARATION

This is what must be done to ensure the test’s accuracy:


  • Urine test: Avoid urinating for two hours before a urine test
  • Vaginal swab: Avoid douching or using vaginal creams for 24 hours prior to the test.
  • Throat swab: No preparation is needed
  • Rectal swab: No preparation is needed
  • Penile swab: No preparation is needed

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How are chlamydia tests done?

    Female vaginal swab

    A doctor will collect a fluid sample from the vagina and/or the cervix using a thin, sterile cotton swab. This may cause mild discomfort. If the area is infected and the mucosa is fragile, swabbing might cause minor spotting afterward, though this is uncommon.

    Remember: You should not use vaginal creams or douches 24 hours before this test.

  • How long does it take to get results?

    • You will usually get the results of a urine test back in three business days.
    • You will usually get the results of a swab test is back in four business days.

  • What do the test results mean?

    Below we will discuss three scenarios for chlamydia test results and what they mean.

    1. Positive chlamydia test

    • If a chlamydia test (swab or urine) is positive, you have chlamydia. You will need to notify your partner and receive treatment.

    2. Negative chlamydia test

    If a chlamydia test is negative, that generally means you do not have chlamydia. However, keep two things in mind:

    • Timing: Has enough time passed after the sexual encounter? Two to three weeks after exposure is the ideal time to test for chlamydia.
    • Accuracy: Different tests have different levels of accuracy. The urine test has 98.3% accuracy, whereas the swab test has 94.3% accuracy.

    3. Inconclusive or equivocal chlamydia test

    If the test results are inconclusive, you will need to take another test. Make sure:

    • The ideal test is performed (swab).
    • The test is performed after the ideal incubation period (two to three weeks).
    • Both an alternative test and the test that had inconclusive results are performed.

  • Can I still get treated without having positive results on hands?

    Yes. This is called empiric treatment, which is when treatment is initiated based on assumptions and clinical experience without a formal diagnosis. Empiric treatment can be provided to the following people:

    • People who have symptoms of chlamydia, including discharge from the genitals, painful and frequent urination, or bleeding from the genitals after sexual intercourse or between menstrual periods.
    • People who have had a sexual encounter with an infected person (even if they do not have symptoms).
    • People who have tested positive for gonorrhea.

    (The incubation period is shorter than chlamydia and it is diagnosed earlier. 24% of heterosexual males and 38% of females who have chlamydia are also positive for gonorrhea.)

    • People who have had a sexual encounter with someone who has chlamydia.
    • People who are anxious about chlamydia. (It is best to avoid unnecessary use of antibiotics; however, if you are worried about your STD status, you can get the medication from your healthcare provider.)

  • Where can I get tested?

    Tests are performed in primary care offices, urgent care clinics, GYN clinics, planned parenthood clinics, free STD centers and our specialized STD clinic .

  • Partner protection

    Chlamydia can affect the oral, genital and rectal areas. If your infected fluids come into contact with your partner’s mucosa, you can transmit chlamydia to them. Below are some ways to prevent this:

    • Do not let your infected area touch anyone else’s skin while you are experiencing symptoms or for seven days after treatment.
    • Do not share sex toys.
    • Wash your hands with soap after touching your infected area.

  • Should I tell my partner I have chlamydia?

    You should notify any sex partners you have had within the last 60 days that you tested positive for chlamydia and suggest that they get tested as well. This can prevent further spread. If you are uncomfortable telling them, we can anonymously notify them on your behalf with a call, text or email.

  • Can I get infected with chlamydia again?

    Yes. This is another reason you should notify your partner(s) and suggest that they get tested and treated if necessary.

  • Will health insurance cover the testing?

    Yes, health insurance usually covers the doctor’s office visit, testing and treatment. If you have a high deductible plan and want to know your exact contribution for the testing, please call your insurance company and find out your contribution for the primary care office visit and chlamydia testing

  • What is the cost without health insurance?

    If you do not have health insurance, the cost of the office visit is $84 cash (or $90 credit) plus $60 for the testing. All tests are performed by certified labs.

  • Who should get tested for oral chlamydia?

    Pharyngeal (oral) chlamydia is a common condition that affects up to 1.4% of men who have sex with men (MSMs) and 3%–4% of heterosexual males and females. According to international guidelines, the oral swab test for chlamydia is recommended for the following people:

    • Those who have symptoms of oral chlamydia (swollen, red pharynx, fever, pus-filled patches on the throat, etc.)
    • Those who have engaged in oral sex with a potentially infected person

    According to the CDC, the oral chlamydia screening test is recommended for the following people:

    • MSMs who have performed oral sex in the last year. Moreover, it is recommended that MSMs who use methamphetamine, have multiple sexual partners, or have a partner with chlamydia get a screening every three to six months.
    • People with HIV

    Remember: Chlamydia bacteria are stored locally (in mucosal cells of the area of exposure), and pharyngeal chlamydia cannot be identified with a genital swab, urine test or blood test.
