AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

People who fall in these categories should be tested for gonorrhea even if they have no symptoms:


  • People with a partner who has been diagnosed with gonorrhea
  • People who’ve had unprotected sex with a new partner
  • People who have sex with multiple partners
  • Homosexual males who are sexually active
  • Females age 25 and younger who are sexually active

Of course, people who have signs and symptoms of gonorrhea should be tested, no matter how much time may have passed since they were exposed to someone with this disease.

WHEN TO GET OFFERED?

People who have gonorrhea symptoms can and should be tested anytime, no matter how short or long a time it has been since they believe they may have contracted gonorrhea.


Gonorrhea testing for people without symptoms can be done as early as 2–7 days after the suspected exposure. Here’s what we know about the accuracy of the various kinds of tests if they’re done two weeks after exposure:


  • Genital swab: 99% accuracy
  • Rectal swab: 99% accuracy
  • Oral swab: 99.4% accuracy
  • Urine test: 79% accuracy

TEST TYPES

Gonorrhea is different from other infections in that it stays in the part of the body where it was contracted—in other words, the point of contact. This means that a negative test of one body part doesn’t mean that the person doesn’t have gonorrhea in another part of the body. All “susceptible” areas that came into contact with a part of a partner’s body that may have been infected with gonorrhea should be tested.


Urine and urethral tests can be done on both males and females. Females can have vaginal swabs. If the contact involved anal sex, the “receiving” person should have an anal swab. And if the contact involved oral sex, the “giver” should have a throat swab.

TEST PREPARATION

For a urine test to be as accurate possible, the patient should not urinate for 2 hours before the test.


Females who are going to have a vaginal swab should not douche or use vaginal cream for 24 hours before the test.


For throat, rectal, and penile swabs, no preparation is necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How is gonorrhea testing done?

    Vaginal swab

    Ideally, this test is performed by a doctor (self-swabbing is possible). A small amount of fluid is collected from the vagina and/or cervix using a thin, sterile cotton swab. See the illustration below.


    This procedure sometimes is very mildly uncomfortable. Rarely, the patient may have minor spotting afterward (this can occur if the area is infected and the mucosa is fragile).


    Important: don’t use vaginal creams and don’t douche within the 24-hour period before the test.

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Urethral swab for males

    A thin, sterile cotton swab is inserted three fourths of an inch into the urethra (from the tip of the penis). The swab collects a small amount of fluid. This test, which is done by a doctor, takes only about 15 to 30 seconds. The procedure may cause mild discomfort.

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Throat swab

    A thin, sterile Q-tip is used to swab the throat.

    Anal swab

    A thin, sterile Q-tip is used to swab the inside of the anus.

    Urine test

    As with other urine tests you’ve probably had, you will be asked to urinate in a cup.

    Important: Do not urinate for at least two hours before the test.

  • How long does it take to get results back?

    The rests of a urine test are usually back in three business days. The results of a swab test usually take four business days.

  • What do the test results mean?

    Test results can positive, negative, or equivocal (inconclusive).

    • Positive

    If your gonorrhea test is positive, you have gonorrhea and need to be treated. You should be retested three weeks after your symptoms resolve. It is also advised that you tell your partner about your diagnosis and suggest they be tested.

    • Negative

    If your gonorrhea test is negative, you may not have gonorrhea. Two factors must be taken into account, though:

    • Timing—Two to three weeks after exposure to gonorrhea is the ideal time to get tested. If you were tested more quickly than that, your test results may not be as accurate
    • The accuracy of the particular test—urine tests are 79% accurate, whereas swab tests are 99% accurate

    • Equivocal/inconclusive

    If your gonorrhea test is equivocal, this means the results aren’t conclusive and repeat testing is needed:

    • The ideal test—a swab test—should be performed
    • The new test should be done 2–3 weeks after the suspected exposure
    • This time, the same test you had before should be repeated and an alternative test should also be done

  • Can I be treated without test results?

    Yes. Treatment can be initiated without a final diagnosis based on the doctor’s clinical experience and assumptions about your condition based on everything he or she has learned about your situation. This is called empiric treatment and there is a consensus in medicine that it can be provided in these any of these circumstances:

    • You have signs and symptoms of gonorrhea, such as genital discharge, painful and frequent urination, burning during urination, or bleeding from the genitals after sexual intercourse or between menstrual periods
    • You had sex with someone who has gonorrhea (even if you have no symptoms)
    • You’ve tested positive for chlamydia
    • (24% of heterosexual males and 38% of females who have chlamydia also test positive for gonorrhea)
    • You had sex with someone who has chlamydia
    • You are very anxious about the possibility of having gonorrhea (it is best not to use antibiotics without an objective reason, but if you’re extremely worried and anxious about this, we will prescribe the medication)

  • Where can I be tested for gonorrhea?

    Primary care offices, urgent care clinics, gynecology clinics, planned parenthood clinics, free STD centers, and our specialized STD clinic are all able to test you for gonorrhea.

  • How can I protect my partner?

    The oral, genital, or rectal area can be infected with gonorrhea. For you to pass gonorrhea to your partner, fluids from your body that are infected with gonorrhea must come into contact with your partner’s mucosa. You can present this by following these recommendations:

    • Don’t let your infected area come into contact with another person’s skin while you have symptoms and for seven days after you’re treated
    • Don’t allow your partner to touch the part of your body that’s infected
    • Don’t share sex toys
    • If you touch the part of your body that’s infected, wash your hands with soap before touching another person

  • Should I tell my partner that I have gonorrhea?

    You should tell everyone you’ve had a sexual encounter with in the last 60 days that you have gonorrhea. Suggest they see a doctor and get tested to prevent the infection from being passed back to you or on to someone else, and to prevent the possibility of health problems due to gonorrhea.


    If you’re not comfortable contacting your sex partners about this, we can notify them on your behalf, anonymously, by phone, email, or text.

  • Can I get gonorrhea again?

    Yes, you can get gonorrhea again after being treated. This is one important reason to tell your partner(s) about your infection; if they’re tested and treated, it’s less likely you’ll get it again from them.

  • Is gonorrhea testing covered by health insurance?

    Yes. Gonorrhea testing is typically covered by health insurance, as many times as you need it, because it’s medically necessary.


    To be certain that it’s covered under your health insurance, call your insurance company and ask what lab they prefer (they may have pricing agreements with certain labs) and whether there is a copay, coinsurance, or deductible that applies, in which case you will have financial responsibility.

  • What does gonorrhea testing cost without health insurance?

    Without health insurance, an office visit is $84 cash (or $90 credit). The testing by a certified lab is $60.

  • Who should get tested for oral or rectal gonorrhea?

    Up to 3% of sexually active persons have pharyngeal (oral) gonorrhea at some point. Under international guidelines, an oral swab test for gonorrhea is recommended to anyone in these circumstances:

    • People who have signs and symptoms of oral gonorrhea
    • (a swollen, red pharynx; fever; pus-filled patches on the throat, etc.)
    • People who are engaging in high-risk behavior (oral sex, multiple partners, etc.)

    According to the CDC, a screening test for oral gonorrhea is recommended for people with HIV and for men having sex with men (MSMs) who have had oral sex in the last year. Additionally, MSMs who use methamphetamine, have multiple sex partners, or have one or more partners doing any of these things should have a screening test every three to six months.


    Important: Gonococcal bacteria remain in the local area of the exposure, so oral gonorrhea cannot be diagnosed using genital swabs, urine tests, and blood tests.
