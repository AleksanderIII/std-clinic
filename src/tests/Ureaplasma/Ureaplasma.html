AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Screening or routine testing


  • The CDC does not recommend routine screening for ureaplasma because it is considered part of the normal microbiome (tiny organisms that live in and on the human body and help with digestion, fighting infection, etc.). However, sometimes these harmless bacteria become too numerous and cause inflammation of healthy tissues.

Females who should be tested


  • Pregnant females
  • Females who have vaginal discharge, odor, or burning that cannot be attributed to any other infection (chlamydia, gonorrhea, mycoplasma, BV , yeast
  • Females with recurrent or resistant BV
  • Females who do not respond to antibiotic treatment of a common infection

Males who should be tested


  • Males need to be tested and possibly treated if their female partner has symptoms caused by ureaplasma
  • Males who have urethritis symptoms and all other bacteria that might cause them are ruled out (chlamydia, gonorrhea, mycoplasma genitalium, and trichomoniasis)

TESTING ACCURACY

The following are the accuracy rates of certain nucleic acid–based tests such as PCR:


  • Vaginal swab: 98.5%
  • Penile swab: 98.7%
  • Urine test: 86.8%

TEST TYPES

Ureaplasma. can be tested with either a swab test or a urine test:


  • Males can a urethral swab or urine test
  • Females can have a vaginal swab or urine test

Both the swab and urine tests are nucleic acid amplification (NAAT) PCR tests.

TEST PREPARATION

For the results of either kind of test to be as accurate as possible, you should follow these instructions:


  • Urine test: do not urinate for 2 hours before the test
  • Vaginal swab: do not douche or use any vaginal cream for 24 hours before the test
  • Penile swab: no special preparation is needed

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How are ureaplasma tests performed?

    Female vaginal swab

    It’s possible for the patient to do the swab herself, but it’s ideal for a doctor to do it.


    A thin, sterile cotton swab is used to collect fluid from the vagina and/or cervix. (See the illustration below.) This might cause very mild discomfort. Minor spotting could occur after the swab if infection is present and the mucosa is fragile, but this is uncommon.


    Remember: don’t use any vaginal cream or douche for 24 hours before the test.

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Male urethral swab

    A doctor inserts a thin, sterile cotton swab into the tip of the penis until it is three fourths of an inch into the urethra. (See the illustration below.) The swab collects fluid for testing. This takes only about 15 to 30 seconds and the procedure may be mildly uncomfortable.

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Urine test (males and females)

    For this test, you merely need to urinate into a cup, catching the first part of your urine stream.


    Remember: do not urinate for at least 2 hours before the test.

  • Can a ureaplasma test be done orally or rectally?

    No, it is not recommended that this testing be done orally or rectally.

  • When will the results be available?

    The results of either a swab test or urine test are usually available in seven business days.

  • If I have ureaplasma, will I need treatment?

    Females who have had more than one sex partner in their lifetime typically have ureaplasma bacteria as part of their normal vaginal flora. Therefore, treatment for ureaplasma will be considered only if other conditions have been ruled out (ureaplasma infection is a diagnosis of exclusion). Treatment for ureaplasma may be necessary in these circumstances:

    • The patient has BV that recurs or is resistant to treatment
    • The patient has a recurring vaginal infection that is not responding to treatment
    • The patient has persistent vaginal burning and/or an odorous discharge
    • The patient has been tested for all common infections and those tests have been negative

  • Are testing and treatment recommended for the sex partners of people who test positive for ureaplasma?

    Yes! Testing and treatment of partners will make treatment of the initial patient’s symptoms more effective.

  • Are females who are positive for ureaplasma considered to be contagious?

    No. Unless ureaplasma is causing symptoms, it is not considered a contagious condition but rather just a normal part of the body.

  • When are testing and treatment of males recommended?

    Testing men who do not have symptoms for ureaplasma is not commonly recommended.


    Males are less likely than females to get ureaplasma. Up to 80% of females and 53% of males, worldwide, have ureaplasma bacteria in their bodies. In the United States, 15% to 40% of sexually active adults with no history of symptoms related to ureaplasma have been found to have ureaplasma bacteria in their bodies.


    Males should be tested for ureaplasma in these circumstances:

    • When they have symptoms of urethritis and other more common infections (chlamydia, gonorrhea, mycoplasma genitalium, and trichomoniasis) have been ruled out
    • They have a partner who has tested positive for ureaplasma

  • Is ureaplasma an STD?

    No, ureaplasma is not a sexually transmitted disease in the classic sense. Testing positive for it does not suggest infidelity. Ureaplasma is part of most females’ normal flora, and it can live in balance (that is, without causing infection or any problems) on the skin of the genitals. It was originally obtained through sex at some point in the person’s life, but when it causes symptoms it is because of overgrowth, not because of sexual activity.

  • How is ureaplasma treated?

    Treatment of ureaplasma depends on the situation and the patient’s allergy profile. Three treatment options are available:

    • A medication from the macrolide group (azithromycin, clarithromycin, or erythromycin)
    • Doxycycline
    • A medication from the fluoroquinolone group (levofloxacin, ofloxacin, or moxifloxocin)

  • Where can I be tested for ureaplasma?

    Ureaplasma testing is not done in a lot of places because it is part of the body’s normal flora. Gynecology clinics, urology clinics, and our specialized STD clinic offer this type of testing.

  • Is it recommended that I notify my sex partner(s) that I have ureaplasma?

    Yes, this is recommended. They should be examined by a doctor and have testing to prevent the infection from being spread further or passed back to you. Health complications are possible although in most people ureaplasma does not cause any symptoms and is not harmful.


    If you wish, we can notify them for you, anonymously, by calling them, sending them a text message, or emailing them.

  • If I’ve had a ureaplasma infection once, can I get it again?

    Yes. This is one the reasons that your sex partner(s) should be tested and treated.ful.

  • When can testing be done again after the treatment for ureaplasma?

    In 3–4 weeks after treatment, you can be retested to find out if the treatment was effective.

  • What are the symptoms of ureaplasma?

    Males


    Ureaplasma cause urethritis in males. The signs and symptoms of urethritis are pain during urination and discharge from the urethra (penis). Ureaplasma may also contribute to prostatitis, which is inflammation of the prostate gland that tends to cause more frequent urination and decreased urine flow.


    Females


    Females with ureaplasma infection may experience lower abdominal pain and/or pain in the genitals. They may also have painful urination and fatigue. Rarely, ureaplasma causes joint pain, persistent urethritis, and abscesses (collections of pus under the skin).


    Importantly, in most cases, people who are positive for ureaplasma have no symptoms, and they could develop more serious health conditions if they go untreated.

  • Does health insurance cover ureaplasma testing?

    Yes, health insurance usually covers the visit to the doctor, testing, and treatment. If you have high-deductible plan and want to know how much you will have to pay out of pocket, you can call your insurance company and ask the amount of your contribution for the primary care office visit and ureaplasma testing.

  • If I don’t have health insurance, how much does testing cost?

    Without health insurance, the office visit is $84 cash (or $90 if paid with a credit card) and the charge for the testing (which will be done by a certified lab) is $60.
