AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Females


  • Women should start screening with the Pap test at age 21.
  • Beginning at age 30, women have three options for screening:
    • 1. A Pap test alone every three years
    • 2. Co-testing with Pap and HPV tests every five years
    • 3. An HPV test alone every five years

Males


  • No screening tests

WHEN TO GET TESTED?

The only body part that can be tested for screening (preventative) purposes is the vagina. Other body parts that can be affected by the HPV virus can be tested only if the patient has symptoms or is considered at high risk for it.


Female genitals


  • Women should start screening with the Pap test at age 21.
  • Beginning at age 30, women have three options for screening:
  • 1. A Pap test alone every three years
  • 2. Co-testing with Pap and HPV tests every five years
  • 3. An HPV test alone every five years

Male genitals


No symptoms


  • No routine screening is advised.

With symptoms


Most visible warts can be diagnosed based on a visual physical exam and the history of the skin lesion.


Throat


No symptoms


  • No routine testing is advised unless the person is considered at very high risk (multiple lifetime oral sex partners).

With symptoms


  • Most visible warts can be diagnosed based on a visual physical exam and the history of the skin lesion. A biopsy can be done if necessary.

Rectal


No symptoms


  • No routine testing is advised unless the person is considered at very high risk (multiple lifetime instances of “rectal receiving”—that is, being a “bottom”)

With symptoms


  • Most visible warts can be diagnosed based on a visual physical exam and the history of the skin lesion. A biopsy can be done if necessary.

TEST TYPES

Testing for HPV is done in different ways, depending on the part of the body that needs to be tested.


  • Female vagina: Pap smear test
  • Male genital skin: Visual physical exam and biopsy if necessary
  • Rectal area: Pap smear or visual exam during anoscopy with possible biopsy
  • Throat area: simple mirror exam of difficult-to-see throat areas

TEST PREPARATION

  • Vaginal: Avoid sexual intercourse, douching, and using vaginal creams for 24 hours before the test
  • Throat swab: No preparation is needed
  • Rectal swab: Avoid anal sex, douching, and enemas 24 hours before the test
  • Skin exam: No preparation is needed

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is known about genital warts?

    • 85% of all people get HPV in their lifetime
    • There are over 100 known HPV viruses
    • 90% of those that get exposed never develop a visible skin lesion
    • The first occurrence or a recurrence can happen months to years after contracting the virus
    • Only 10% of those infected can transmit the virus to other people
    • There is a 70% chance of getting HPV from an infected partner even without visible skin lesions
    • 90% of HPV infections are cleared by the immune system but some will remain latent (that is, have no visible signs) for the rest of the person’s life
    • HPV types 6 and 11 cause 70% of all visible genital warts
    • HPV 16 and 18 cause 50% of cervical cancers in females

  • How does HPV transmission occur?

    • HPV is transmitted through direct skin-to-skin contact. The infected skin or mucosa comes into contact with healthy skin and enters it through micro abrasions
    • The presence of visible warts is not necessary for transmission to occur, but visible warts have the highest concentration of the virus and increase the chance of transmission
    • Once acquired, the HPV infection can develop into a visible wart or enter a latent state with no sign and symptoms
    • The average time for the wart’s appearance is 2.9 months

  • What are the risk factors for getting HPV?

    • Sexual activity—simple skin-to-skin contact—is the primary risk factor for genital warts.
    • Here are some other factors that can predispose a person to HPV:
      • Weakened immune system due to HIV, diabetes, immunosuppressive medications, etc.
      • Smoking
    • Male circumcision, on the other side, may reduce the risk of HPV infection.

  • What do HPV lesions look like?

    The appearance of genital warts varies from person to person.


    Shape


    Genital warts are single or multiple flat, dome-shaped, or cauliflower-shaped skin growths.


    Surface


    The surface may be smooth, velvety on a broad base.


    Color


    The color may vary from pale pink to white to skin-colored to brown.


    Size


    A wart can be from 1 mm to 1 inch in diameter.

  • What should I know about treatment for genital warts?

    What is the best treatment for genital warts?

    The “best” treatment for genital warts depends on the number of warts, their location, other health conditions you might have, and your doctor’s experience with different treatment types and preference. There are many ways to treat genital warts, including topical medications and removing them with a procedure in the doctor’s office.

    Self-treatment options

    Two prescription medication are commonly used:

    Podofilox (Condylox)

    How it works

    It destroys the wart tissue.


    How it’s used

    The medication is applied with a cotton swab to visible warts twice a day for three days in a row. Then for four days there is no treatment.


    Treatment duration

    Treatment should be continued until the warts are gone. This might take up to one month. Side effects include soreness and significant skin irritation.

    Imiquimod (Aldara)

    How it works

    It triggers the immune system to get rid of the warts.


    How is it used?

    It is applied on the wart three days a week at bedtime and washed off in the morning.


    Treatment duration

    Treatment might take up to 16 weeks.

    Doctor’s office treatment options

    In a doctor’s office, genital warts can be destroyed by freezing, burning, or surgically removing them.


    Trichloracetic acid (TCA) is an acid that destroys visible warts. It is applied by a doctor weekly for 3–4 weeks until the warts are gone. Side effects involve burning and pain. TCA treatment is not advised on the vagina, vulva, cervix, or urethral opening. TCA is not as effective as cryotherapy treatment.


    Cryotherapy refers to the use of liquid nitrogen to destroy the warts. This is the most common method because of its effectiveness and simplicity. Side effects might involve skin irritation, blistering, and sores that heal within a week.


    Electrocautery uses electrical energy to destroy the warts. It’s a bit more painful than cryotherapy and is usually done with local anesthesia.


    Laser treatment uses light energy to remove the warts. Specialized equipment is necessary and for that reason this treatment is more expensive and not very common. Laser treatment’s most common side effects are pain, scarring, and depigmentation (lightening) of the skin.


    Surgical removal (excision) is done mainly for

    • Warts that don’t get better with medical therapy,
    • Large warts when medical therapy is not going to be adequate, or
    • Areas with precancerous changes.

    Are genital warts curable?

    Regardless of the treatment type, warts might come back within a few weeks to months after the treatment. This is because treatment of the visible warts might not get rid of all of the HPV virus. Some of the skin cells of the genitals might look normal but remain infected with HPV, and they could be far from the original area.


    Currently, there is no treatment that permanently removes HPV from all infected cells, but in most people the immune system clears the virus within two years (there’s no test to confirm that this has occurred).

    Does everybody need to be treated for HPV?

    Warts don’t necessarily have to be treated especially if they’re not bothersome. Most people get treatment to decrease the spread to other parts of their own body and to partners, since visible warts have a higher concentration of the virus than normal skin.


    Warts might progress initially, so the number of warts and their size might increase. But it’s estimated that about 30% of genital warts get better within four months.

    What causes reoccurrence of genital warts?

    The HPV virus might persist despite the resolution of visible warts. This happens because some of the virus remains latent (inactive) in skin cells without creating any visible skin lesions.


    The reoccurrence rate is not defined.


    The most common causes of reoccurrence are believed to be mechanical irritation, inflammation, or immunosuppression that causes the virus’s reactivation (replication) and the reappearance of warts.

  • What should I know about treatment for anal (rectal) warts?

    Who should be screened for anal HPV?

    Screening for anal HPV is not common. It is reserved for the following categories of people:

    • Men who have sex with men
    • Woman with a history of high-grade cervical (vulvar, vaginal) cancer
    • People with a history of anal warts

    How often is an anal Pap smear performed?

    Anal screening for high-risk people is advised every two to three years.

    How accurate is an anal Pap smear for detecting HPV?

    The accuracy and sensitivity of the anal Pap smear is similar to the cervical Pap smear that is regularly performed on females: around 50%.

    What is the next step if an anal Pap smear is abnormal?

    A Pap smear detects abnormal cells. If abnormal cells are detected on a Pap smear, the next step is to visualize the area and take a biopsy to assess the severity of the disease.

    What is the chance of anal HPV being malignant?

    Most anal warts (as well as other genital warts) are caused by HPV 6 and 11 and are considered low risk for malignancy, but they may coinfect with other higher-risk strains (16 and 18) that can lead to cancer.


    Cancerous transformation of genital warts is very rare. People with a weakened immune system (due to cancer, HIV, or another immunosuppressive condition) may be predisposed to cancerous transformation.

  • What should I know about treatment for oral or throat HPV?

    How is oral HPV diagnosed?

    There is no test that can find early signs of HPV in the throat. It is most commonly detected during a routine dental exam or when there are symptoms and signs.


    To diagnose asymptomatic oral HPV, doctors do a visual physical exam and use a small mirror to check difficult-to-see areas (but this is not commonly done).


    Some structures of the throat that cannot be seen with a mirror can be accessed with a flexible laryngoscope by an ear, nose, and threat (ENT) physician.


    If an HPV lesion is noticed, it will be treated by removal and can be biopsied to rule out cancer if it is suspicious.

    What is the best way to be protected from oral HPV?

    The only way to be protected from oral HPV is to not have sex.


    The HPV vaccine that was developed to reduce the risk of cervical cancer can reduce the risk of oral and throat HPV as well. Ideally, the vaccine should be given before the beginning of sexual activity, but it can be somewhat helpful at any age.

  • Can HPV (genital warts) be prevented?

    There is no sure way to prevent getting HPV.


    But there are two common ways of decreasing the chance of getting HPV:

    • HPV vaccine
    • Reducing the number of sexual partners

    Let’s talk about them in more detail!

    Vaccination

    Vaccines are given not to treat but to prevent the condition, and ideally (for the best results) should be given before the beginning of the patient’s sex life.

    • Two HPV vaccines are available: Gardasil and Gardasil 9.
      • Gardasil protects against four HPV types: 6, 11, 16, and 18.
      • Gardasil 9 protects against nine virus types: 6, 11, 16, and 18 plus five additional strains: HPV-31, HPV-33, HPV-45, HPV-52, and HPV-58.

        • Note: Types 16 and 18 cause an estimated 70% of cervical cancer. Strains HPV-31, HPV-33, HPV-45, HPV-52, and HPV-58 are responsible for 20% of cervical cancers.

    • Both vaccines are safe.
    • These vaccines can be given at any age, but ideally between the ages of 9 and 12.
    • Here are the current recommendations for the HPV vaccine:
      • Females 9–26 years of age and up to age 45
      • Males 11–12 and up to age 21; from ages 22 to 26, the vaccine is considered optional

        • Note: If the vaccine is not given at the ideal age, the response might be partial since the patient might have already been exposed to some common HPV strains. The vaccine is not harmful, regardless.

    • Health insurance covers the cost of the vaccine until the age of 45.

  • Can I protect my partner from HPV?

    Sexual activity and HPV

    Sex—especially penetrative sex—is the primary risk factor for getting HPV. Avoiding people with HPV would be ideal, but from a practical standpoint this is impossible to do since most people positive for HPV don’t have visible skin lesions; rather, their infection is latent, and they can still spread the infection with skin-to-skin contact.


    Condoms provide only partial protection since they don’t cover the entire skin area that might come into contact with an infected partner’s skin.

    How can I talk with my partner about HPV?

    If you have had visible genital warts (that were treated or got better on their own) in the past, have an honest talk with your partner before sexual activity.


    Tell your partner these three main points about HPV:

    • It is a common condition and most people don’t have visible signs
    • There is no test to look for genital warts on the skin
    • The test that is routinely performed for females is called a Pap smear; it checks for different wart types

    What’s the connection between HPV and infidelity?

    There is no clear-cut connection between HPV and cheating while in a relationship. We do not advise people to apologize for having HPV or to blame their partner for having HPV. And here’s why:

    • HPV is a very common infection, and 85% of people get it at some point in their lifetime
    • It is one of the risks people face by being sexually active
    • Unlike other STDs, an HPV infection might remain latent (with no visible signs) for months or even years before showing up as a wart (and only 10% of positive people ever have symptoms)
    • This means that if your partner has HPV and didn’t get it from you, they might have gotten it long before they met you
    • Since sexual partners can share the same virus, it is always difficult to say where the infection originated, anyway
    • Even if both partners are positive, only one may be symptomatic, and it’s not necessarily the one who contracted the virus first

    What is the connection between HPV and cancer?

    Not all HPV leads to cancer. This is a common misconception. Of more than 100 HPV strains, only a handful are connected to cancer. It is true that HPV (some strains) can cause cancer, but it is a very unusual complication.


    The HPV vaccine significantly decreases the chance of cancer caused by cancerous strains of HPV for cervical cancer (which only people with a vagina can get) and rectal cancer.
