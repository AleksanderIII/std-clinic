AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

  • Routine

    • The CDC does not recommend routine herpes testing

  • Post-exposure testing

    • If you have been exposed to a herpes-positive person and want to know your status, you should be tested three weeks after the exposure

  • When you have symptoms

    • Of course, if you have symptoms and your condition needs to be diagnosed, you should be tested

WHEN TO GET TESTED?

No symptoms


Routine


  • A routine IgG test can be done at any time (if you’re not aware of a specific exposure)

Post-exposure testing


  • If you don’t have symptoms, only an IgG blood test can be done
    • 3 weeks after exposure: 50% accuracy
    • 6 weeks after exposure: 70% accuracy
    • 12 weeks after exposure: 92% accuracy

When you have symptoms


  • When the first blisters appear, a swab test can be done
  • The swab test requires that a sample be collected from a herpes lesion; it is not performed when no lesions are present. There is a very short window of time to collect a sample, so testing should not be delayed

TEST TYPES

Herpes swab or culture test


  • Accuracy: 100%
  • Chance of a false positive result: This depends on when the sample is collected; the chance is most commonly 25%, but a false positive may be up to 70% likely if the sample is not collected at the proper time (again, there is a very short window for sample collection)

IgG Ab test


  • Accuracy: 94%
  • Chance of a false positive result: 19%
  • Chance of a false negative result: 38%

Washington University test (western blot test)


  • Accuracy: 99%
  • Chance of a false positive result: 0%
  • Chance of a false negative result: 0%
  • This test is the gold standard herpes blood test. “Western blot” is the name of the test and Washington University is where it’s done. False negative and false positive rates for the IgG test, which is commercially available, are determined in comparison to western blot results

TEST PREPARATION

Swab or culture test


  • Do not break the blisters
  • Do not apply any cream 24 hours before testing
  • Do not wipe the oozing sores

IgG Ab test


  • No special preparation is necessary for this simple blood test

Washington University test (western blot test)


This test is done only in the Virology Department of the University of Washington in Seattle. The entire process may take more than a month. It involves the following steps:


  • Contacting the university and obtain a test kit
  • See a doctor to have blood drawn
  • Return the kit to Washington University

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How should I choose the right herpes test?

    To determine the type of test you need, two questions must be answered:

    • Do you currently have symptoms?
    • If not, how long ago were you exposed to a herpes-positive person?

    If you have symptoms

    • If you have a skin blister or sore that appears to be herpes, you can get the most accurate test, a culture test
    • These limitations apply to a culture test:
      • You must have a visible sore
      • Ideally, the test is performed within 24 hours of the appearance of blisters (if necessary, it can be done within 48 hours)
      • It is best for your herpes blister to be intact and broken only by the health provider at the time of the test
      • The sore must be wet so a swab can be done (dry, crusted sores cannot be swabbed)

    If you do not have symptoms

    • If at least three weeks have passed since your exposure (but ideally, three months), you can get a herpes IgG antibody test
    • With no symptoms, a swab test can’t be done because there is nothing to swab

  • What do herpes test results mean?

    When your herpes test results are available, they must be interpreted. This is not necessarily straightforward.


    In this table, we summarize what positive and negative results for each test mean—assuming the test is done at the right time (see above).

    Test Positive Negative
    Culture You can be 100% certain that your skin lesion is caused by herpes
    • The skin that was swabbed is probably not a herpes lesion. This doesn’t mean that you don’t have latent (symptomless) herpes. Being positive for herpes on a blood test and having a herpes skin outbreak are two different things
    • False-negative results are possible: 25% of herpes swab tests are considered false negatives, which occurs when not enough sample was collected
    Commercially available IgG
    • You are most likely herpes positive
    • There is a 19% chance of false-positive result—this means that about 19% of people who test negative actually have herpes
    • You are considered herpes negative
    • False-negative results are possible: there is about a 38% chance of this (you are herpes positive, but the test didn’t pick it up)
    Washington University HSV You almost certainly (>99%) have herpes You almost certainly (>99%) do not have herpes

  • Can I get herpes treatment?

    Herpes treatment is given in three situations:

    • The patient has a current herpes outbreak
    • The patient has frequent herpes outbreaks
    • The patient wants to decrease the risk of passing herpes to a partner

    Treatment regimen for the first herpes outbreak

    • The first herpes outbreak is the most severe, with more discomfort and more skin lesions, and it lasts the longest
    • For that reason, treatment for the first outbreak involves a higher dose of medication and a longer course of treatment than treatment for later outbreaks

    Treatment regimen for recurrent herpes outbreaks

    Recurrent outbreaks are any outbreak after the first one.

    • Outbreaks are more common with herpes 1 than with herpes 2
      • HSV-1: 25% chance
      • HSV-2: 60%–90 % chance
    • Outbreaks are more common during the first few years

    The treatment regimen (dose and duration of medication) depend on the anatomic location of the outbreak, not the herpes type.

    “Suppressive” herpes medication to reduce frequent outbreaks

    Very few herpes-positive people have frequent (more than five a year) herpes outbreaks. Medication reduces the frequency of outbreaks by 70%–80%.


    Daily antiviral therapy is recommended for people who have frequent herpes outbreaks. Your doctor should reevaluate your treatment 6 to 12 months after it begins.

  • Is it important to know whether I have HSV-1 or HSV-2?

    No, it is not important for you and your doctor to know which type of herpes you have because this knowledge won’t change the treatment plan or your prognosis (expected outcome).


    Although there are some differences (for example, type 2 tends to cause more frequent outbreaks and be transmitted more easily, especially during the first three months after exposure), the treatment plan and prognosis are the same for both types:

    • Most people have a great long-term outcome. The exception can be people whose immune systems are suppressed (for example, people with HIV and people undergoing chemotherapy), who can develop serious complications from herpes of either type
    • Recurrent herpes treatment is based on the anatomic location of the virus in the body rather than the type of herpes (as you may recall, both herpes types can affect either the oral or genital area of the body)

    Herpes stigma


    Although we know that both types of herpes may be transmitted orally or genitally and can affect virgins and people with only one sexual partner, some people feel stigmatized if they learn that they have HSV-2 because it is considered an STD and is commonly called “genital herpes.”

  • How long does it take to get test results?

    • IgG test: 3 to 4 business days
    • Washington University Western blot test: one month or longer
    • Swab or culture test: 4 to 5 business days

    This test is performed only at the University of Washington Virology Department, which makes this test less available to the general public. It is usually not covered by health insurance.

  • What should I be aware of if I want to protect my partner from getting herpes?

    It’s not possible to eliminate the risk of giving your partner herpes because all preventive measures offer only partial protection. However, there are ways to significantly reduce the risk:


    Avoid sex and kissing during outbreaks


    This is when you are most contagious because your fluid-filled blisters and sores have a high concertation of herpes virus.


    Pay attention to prodrome symptoms


    Prodrome herpes symptoms are usually very nonspecific symptoms (meaning they can occur with many health conditions), such as fatigue, mild skin tingling, and shooting pain where the outbreaks usually occur (in the oral or genital area). Herpes virus is likely to shed during that period.


    Avoid skin trauma during sexual intercourse


    Damaged skin is more vulnerable to herpes infection. Use silicon-based lubricant during vaginal or anal sex.


    Maintain proper hand hygiene


    The herpes virus may stay on your hand after you touch your blisters or sores, and you can easily transmit the infection if you touch your partner. Always wash your hands with soap after you touch your herpes lesions.


    Do not share utensils during oral herpes outbreaks


    There is a theoretical risk of herpes transmission via utensils since it is possible to exchange virus-containing fluids via utensils, bringing the skin around the mouth or the oral mucosa into contact with the virus. The likelihood of this happening is unknown and considered extremely low.


    Use condoms


    Condoms may decrease the chance of herpes transmission (per multiple sex acts) by 50%. However, the parts of the genitals that are not covered by condoms will still be exposed to the virus.


    Take antiviral medication


    Appropriate antiviral therapy cuts the risk of herpes transmission (whether you have symptoms or not) in half.

  • How can herpes be transmitted if I don’t have symptoms?

    The fact that it is possible to pass herpes when you don’t have symptoms makes protecting your partner complicated. People want to be ethical and protect their partner, but it’s not always possible.


    Many unanswered questions remain despite asymptomatic herpes transmission having been researched. We still must make a lot of educated assumptions.


    Here’s what’s known about asymptomatic herpes transmission (“silent virus shedding”):


    How it occurs


    Asymptomatic herpes transmission is believed to happen this way: Activated herpes virus from a local nerve ganglion travels to the surface of the skin. Transmission occurs with skin-to-skin contact where the virus is active. It is still unclear how high a concentration of virus must be on the skin for transmission to occur.


    When it occurs


    This is not completely clear. It is believed that the same factors that cause flare-ups (stress, exhaustion, and disease) can increase the likelihood of asymptomatic transmission. And it is believed that when this occurs, it’s because the patient’s immune system strong enough to suppress the virus quickly, preventing visible skin lesions.


    Riskiest time

    • Transmission rates are highest in the first year after infection
    • Transmission is most likely in the few days before and after an outbreak

  • Does health insurance cover testing?

    The commercially available IgG antibody testing and the culture swab test are covered by health insurance. The Washington University western blot test is not.


    If you want to be sure that your particular insurance covers the testing, call your insurance company and ask:


    1. The name of their preferred lab (e.g., Quest, Labcorp, Northwell)—they probably have contractual pricing arrangements with certain labs

    2. Whether a herpes culture test and herpes IgG test are covered


    If the insurance company representative asks you for ICD and CPT codes, give them these:

    • ICD B00.9
    • CPT 86695 or 86696

    3. Ask what the patient responsibility is for the test (after a deductible or coinsurance)

  • Where can I be tested for herpes?

    The testing can be done in primary care offices, urgent care clinics, gynecology clinics, planned parenthood clinics, and our specialized STD clinic. Free STD clinics do not perform herpes blood testing since it is not recommended by the CDC.
