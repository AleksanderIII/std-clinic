AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Only a person with a penis can get Balanitis.



Screening or routine test

Not necessary


Males:

With symptoms of itchiness, red spots, whitish discharge, and fishy smell

TEST TYPES AVAILABLE

  • Physical examination in the doctor’s office:

    • Most Balanitis diagnoses are established during a physical exam, just by examining the area since most of the symptoms are visible.

  • Swab

    • Can be performed if there is discharge.

  • Biopsy

    • Biopsy is performed for a chronic fungal infection.

TEST PREPARATION

  • Most of the time, no test preparation is necessary since diagnosis can be established by visual examination.
  • Stop using creams 24 hours prior to examination since swabbing of the penile skin surface might be necessary.

TREATMENT FOR BALANITIS

Treatment depends on the cause but mostly entails clotrimazole and miconazole topical cream.


Frequently Asked Questions

  • How are different Balanitis tests performed?

    Office examination

    The office evaluation consists of looking at the penile skin, assessing the extent of the infection, and looking for the potential cause of Balanitis.

    Male penile surface swab

    Ideally, the test is performed by a doctor. Self-swabbing is possible. A sample of discharge from the surface of the penis will be collected by a thin sterile cotton swab.


    Remember: You should not use any creams 24 hours before being tested.

  • How long does it take to get results?

    If the swab test is performed, which is not very common, then results are usually back in 4 business days.

  • What treatments are available?

    If another infection is promoting Balanitis by keeping the area wet, the treatment should be directed at the primary cause.

    Causative factor Treatment option

    Yeast infection (Candida)

    		 Clotrimazole (1%) or Miconazole (2%) cream 2-3 times a day for 10 days

    Herpes Simplex Virus

    		 Acyclovir (400mg) oral tablets 3 times in a day during 7-10 days
    OR
    Acyclovir (200mg) oral tablets 5 times a day during 7-10 days

    Chlamydia Trachomatis

    		 A single-dose of Azithromycin (1g) oral tablets
    OR
    Doxycycline (100mg) oral tablets 2 times a day for a week

    Human Papillomavirus

    		 Podophyllotoxin (0.5%) application twice a day for 3 days
    OR
    Cryotherapy performed by a doctor
    OR
    Surgical removal of the lesions

    Gardnerella Vaginalis

    		 Metronidazole (500mg) oral tablets twice a day for a week
    OR
    Metronidazole gel (0.75%) application once a day for 5 days
    OR
    Clindamycin (2%) cream every night for a week

    Treponema Pallidum

    		 A single dose of an intramuscular injection of Benzathine Penicillin (2.4 MU)

    Trichomonas Vaginalis

    		 A single-dose of Metronidazole (2g) oral tablets
    OR
    Metronidazole (400mg) oral tablets 2 times a day for a week

    If Balanitis resulted from a non-infectious factor (check the list below), maintenance of proper genital hygiene is recommended. Washing the affected area with saline solution (twice a day) is also suggested. Patients should not use any type of aromatized lotions and soaps. Those who have chronic illnesses (diabetes, heart and kidney disease, reactive arthritis) that might be the reason for the Balanitis development should discuss possible disease management options with their doctors.

  • How soon does the treatment work?

    Balanitis has an excellent prognosis. It is most important to identify the causative factor and start the treatment as soon as possible. Usually, patients start to feel better within 3-5 days of treatment onset.

  • Should sexual partners of those who tested positive be tested and treated?

    If the primary cause of Balanitis is another infection, the sexual partner(s) of the patient are recommended to get tested for the corresponding infection and undergo treatment. Otherwise, it is not recommended to treat or test the partners of people diagnosed with Balanitis.

  • Is it necessary to get tested after treatment?

    After-treatment testing is recommended only for those individuals who continue to experience symptoms of Balanitis infection.

  • What is the recommended treatment for Balanitis recurrence?

    The chance of Balanitis recurrence among uncircumcised males is roughly 0.7 %. None of the circumcised men developed a Balanitis recurrence within a 1.5 year follow-up period.

    The primary cause of Balanitis needs to be determined and addressed. Circumcision can be one of the options for severe and recurrent Balanitis. However, circumcision should not be performed during the active inflammation stage of the disease.

  • What are the symptoms of Balanitis?

    The list of Balanitis symptoms and signs includes:

    • Pain and/or tenderness of the head of the penis
    • Penile discharge (white or yellowish)
    • Itching
    • Ulcers on the head of the penis (in case of reactive arthritis or Herpes Simplex Virus)
    • An inflamed and/or swollen head of the penis
    • Painful urination (if the urethra is involved in the process)
    • Rash on the head of the penis (red or white)

  • How is it transmitted?

    The health condition itself (Balanitis) may not be transmitted. In the majority of cases, Balanitis results from the excessive growth of the Candida (a fungus that is a part of the genital microflora). Other underlying reasons may include stress, suppressed immunity, antibiotic use, or trauma.

    However, sometimes Balanitis results from infections that are transmitted through genital, oral and anal sex. For instance:

    • Herpes Simplex Virus
    • Chlamydia Trachomatis
    • Human Papillomavirus
    • Gardnerella Vaginalis
    • Treponema Pallidum
    • Trichomonas Vaginalis

    Also, several non-infectious factors may increase the chance of Balanitis development. Some of them are:

    • Poor genital hygiene
    • Having tight foreskin
    • Washing the genitals with aromatized soaps and lotions
    • Uncontrolled diabetes
    • Obesity
    • Reactive arthritis
    • Allergy to condom latex and/or lubricants
    • Corticosteroids
    • Heart and kidney diseases

  • Where can I get tested or treated for a Balanitis infection?

    The test can be performed in OB/GYN clinics, primary care offices, dermatology clinics or our specialized STD clinic.
