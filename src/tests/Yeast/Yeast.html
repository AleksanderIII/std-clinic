AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Only a person with a vagina can be tested for a yeast infection.


Screening or routine test


  • Not necessary

Females:


  • With symptoms of vaginal discharge, odor, or burning sensation

HOW ACCURATE TEST?

  • Office examination and microscopy: 32%
  • Rapid yeast test: 73%
  • Female vaginal swab PCR: 95%
  • pH test of the vaginal fluid: Generally tests the vaginal pH, not for a yeast infection specifically. The test mainly differentiates between BV (bacterial vaginosis) and a yeast infection based on the pH value
    • Yeast infection pH: 4.0-4.5
    • BV pH: > 4.5

TEST TYPES

Two types of yeast infection tests are available: vaginal swab for PCR and office examination with microscopy.

TEST PREPARATION

For the most accurate testing:


  • Vaginal swab: Avoid having sex, douching and using vaginal cream for 24 hours prior to testing

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How are different yeast infection tests performed?

    Female vaginal swab

    We recommend that a doctor performs the test, but self-swabbing is also possible. A sample of vaginal and/or cervical fluid is collected by a cotton swab (the procedure is illustrated below).


    The procedure may cause mild discomfort or minor spotting, which is rare.


    Remember: Do not use vaginal creams or practice douching 24 hours before testing

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Office examination

    The office examination includes vaginal swabbing, assessing the pH of the swab and performing microscopy.

  • How long does it take to get results?

    Swab test results are usually back in 4 business days.

  • What should I do if my test result is positive?

    You will be advised to take either over-the-counter or oral prescription medication.

  • What treatments are available?

    Treatment depends on the frequency of infection:

    Initial treatment

    A one-time dose of oral Fluconazole (150 mg) will maintain its high concentration in vaginal secretions for 3 days.

    Recurrent treatment

    A recurrent yeast infection is defined as more than 4 episodes per year. A vaginal culture test for different Candida species needs to be performed to identify less common species.


    A regimen of three doses of oral Fluconazole every 72 hours followed by a weekly dose of Fluconazole (150 mg) for 6 months is recommended.

  • How soon will the treatment work?

    Most yeast infections, especially those that are not recurrent, respond to treatment within 2 days. While on medication, there are no contraindications for sexual activity, unless you feel uncomfortable due to inflammation. Treatment for sexual partners is not necessary.

  • If I tested positive, should my sexual partner(s) be tested and treated?

    It is not recommended to treat partners of females diagnosed with a yeast infection.

  • Is it necessary to get tested after treatment?

    After-treatment testing is recommended only for individuals who continue to experience symptoms of a yeast infection.

  • What are contributing factors to a yeast infection?

    Occasional yeast infections can happen to any female for various reasons.


    Some known factors that predispose a female to recurrent yeast infections:

    • Diabetes, especially if sugar levels are not well-controlled.
    • Use of antibiotics. At least 30% of females get yeast infection during or after taking a broad-spectrum antibiotic.
    • Birth control pills with high estrogen levels.
    • Immunosuppressive status (e.g., due to steroid use, HIV, cancer).

  • Do probiotics and other dietary changes lower my risk of yeast infections?

    There is no evidence that yeast infections are caused by a lack of lactobacilli in vaginal secretions. Therefore, probiotic lactobacilli is not recommended.


    Although there is a popular belief that ingestion and vaginal administration of yogurt decrease the risk of infection, there is no evidence based on multiple studies.

  • What behavioral changes lower my risk of yeast infections?

    Some behavioral changes may lower your lower, such as avoiding panty liners, pantyhose, and lubricants. Drinking cranberry juice may be beneficial for some females, but this has not been supported by studies.

  • How do contraceptives affect my risk of yeast infections?

    Oral contraceptive pills

    There is a split in opinions among researchers about the connection between birth control pills (BCP) and yeast infection. Larger and newer studies demonstrate a weak connection.

    Contraceptive devices

    Use of intrauterine devices (IUDs), sponges and diaphragms has been associated with yeast infections. The connection is most likely due to the ability of the Candida (yeast infection fungi) species to stay attached to these devices, not the intrinsic property of these devices.

  • Can a yeast infection be sexually transmitted?

    A yeast infection is not considered a sexually transmitted disease since it can affect virgin females, as Candida is a normal part of the vaginal flora.


    However, this does not mean that yeast infections are not associated with sexual activity. Keep in mind:

    • Increased sexual activity can increase the chances of a yeast infection.
    • The number of sexual partners is not correlated with frequency of yeast infections.
    • Females who have sex with females do not get yeast infections as often as females who have sex with males.

  • Where can I get tested for a yeast infection?

    Testing can be performed in OB/GYN clinics, primary care offices or our specialized STD clinic.

  • Will health insurance cover the testing?

    Yes. Health insurance usually covers the doctor’s office visit, testing and treatment.


    If you have a high deductible plan and want to know your exact contribution for the testing, call your insurance company and find out your contribution for the primary care office visit and yeast infection testing (ICD 73.88; CPT 87491 or 87591).

  • What is the cost of testing without health insurance?

    Without health insurance, the cost of the office visit is $84 cash (or $90 credit) plus $60 for the testing. All testing is performed in certified labs.

  • What are the symptoms of a yeast infection?

    • Itching of the vulva (this is the most common symptom)
    • Pain, redness, or irritation of the vulva and vagina
    • Pain during urination
    • Pain during sex
    • Abnormal vaginal discharge (thick and white, or thin and watery)
