AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

HIV testing is important and should be done if you have early HIV symptoms or are at high risk for HIV.


Symptoms


HIV, in its early stages, can present with many nonspecific symptoms, so the threshold for suspicion should be very low. These nonspecific symptoms include the following:


  • Fever with other nonspecific symptoms
  • Skin rash and fever
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Enlarged lymph nodes
  • Diarrhea
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss

High-risk persons


If you had high-risk contact, you should be screened for HIV, and follow-up testing should be done 4–6 months later (the timing depends on the screening test results). The following persons are considered to be at high risk:


  • MSMs
  • Users of injected drugs
  • Sex workers and their clients
  • People with multiple sex partners
  • People practicing unprotected sex
  • Prisoners
  • Transgender people

WHEN TO GET TESTED?

Different types of HIV tests should be done at different times:


  • The ultrasensitive HIV RNA PCR test used for early detection is sensitive 5 days after exposure
  • The HIV RNA PCR test can be done 10 days after exposure
  • The HIV fourth-generation test (Ab/Ag) can be done as early as 13 days after exposure
  • The HIV Ab test (third-generation) test can be done as early as 22 days after exposure

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is the best screening HIV test?

    Combining the fourth-generation test with the Ag/Ab test is best for screening for HIV.

    • This test is the best at finding early HIV and chronic HIV
    • If the results are positive, follow-up testing should be done using the HIV 1 and 2 Ab test and the HIV RNA PCR test

  • How are different HIV tests done?

    Most HIV tests are blood tests.

  • How long does it take to get the test results back?

    All HIV tests results are back 3–5 business days after blood is drawn.

  • Where can I be tested?

    HIV testing can be done in primary care offices, urgent care clinics, gynecology clinics, planned parenthood clinics, free STD centers, and our specialized STD clinic.

  • Can I protect my partner? How?

    HIV is passed to another person when fluid containing a high concentration of the HIV virus comes into contact with the bloodstream of the other person (who is HIV negative).

    What kind of fluids?

    • Blood
    • Semen (cum)
    • Pre-seminal fluid (cum)
    • Rectal fluid
    • Vaginal fluid
    • Breast milk

    How does this contact with the other person’s bloodstream occur?

    • Through damaged skin
    • Through wounds
    • Through mucous membranes

  • Does health insurance cover the cost of testing?

    Yes, all commercially available HIV tests are covered by health insurance.


    If you want to be certain that your insurance covers it, call your insurance company and ask them these questions:


    1. What lab they prefer for you to use (insurance companies have contracts with certain labs for special pricing; examples of labs are Quest, Labcorp, and Northwell)

    2. Whether the test you want is covered


    If they ask you for ICD and CPT codes, give them these:

    • ICD B00.9
    • CPT 86695 or 86696

    3. What your financial responsibility will be for the test (after any deductible or coinsurance)

  • What does testing cost if I don’t have health insurance?

    If you don’t have health insurance, the office visit charge is $84 cash (or $90 credit), and the testing is an additional $60 (all testing is done at certified labs).
