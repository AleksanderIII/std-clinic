AT A GLANCE

WHO SHOULD GET TESTED?

Most Trichomonas vaginalis infections are asymptomatic.


The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends Trichomonas testing for:


Females


  • With new sexual partners
  • With multiple sexual partners
  • Who are pregnant
  • Who are HIV-positive

Males


  • Routine screening is not recommended

WHEN TO GET TESTED?

Since most T. vaginalis infections are asymptomatic, the exact incubation period is unknown. The estimated incubation period is 5-28 days.


No symptoms


  • Testing is recommended within 30 days of exposure.

With symptoms


  • Testing can be done at any time, irrespective of the amount of time passed since the sexual encounter that you believe may have given you Trichomoniasis.
  • A vaginal swab can be performed as early as 5 days after exposure. If performed 4 weeks after exposure, the accuracy rate is 96%.
  • A penile swab can be performed as early as 5 days after exposure. If performed 4 weeks after exposure, the accuracy rate is 96%.
  • A urine test can be performed as early as 5 days after exposure. If performed 4 weeks after exposure, the accuracy rate is 64-75%.

TEST TYPES

Two types of Trichomonas tests are available: swab and urine tests.


  • Male
    • Urine test and urethral swab
  • Female
    • Urine test and vaginal swab

Both tests (swab and urine) are NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests) PCR tests. However, the test accuracy is higher for swab (96%) than for urine (64%-75%).

TEST PREPARATION

For the most accurate testing:


  • Urine test: Avoid urinating for 2 hours prior
  • Vaginal swab: Avoid douching and using vaginal creams for 24 hours prior
  • Penile swab: No preparation needed

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What are the different T.vaginalis (“Trichomonas” or “Trich”) tests?

    Female vaginal swab

    A doctor will insert a cotton swab to collect vaginal and/or cervical fluid (the procedure is illustrated below). Self-swabbing is also possible. The procedure might cause mild discomfort. Minor spotting afterwards is possible, but uncommon.


    Remember: You should not practice douching or use vaginal creams for 24 hours prior to testing.

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Male urethral swab

    A doctor will insert a cotton swab into the tip of the penis and three-fourths of an inch into the urethra to collect urethral fluid. The test might cause mild discomfort, but is quick (15-30 seconds).

    Image source:
    Chlamydia / N. Gonorrhoeae RNA, TMA - Unisex Collection | Diagnostic Laboratory of Oklahoma. https://www.dlolab.com/chlamydia-n-gonorrhoeae-rna-tma-unisex-collection.

    Urine test

    You will collect first-catch urine (the initial portion of the urine stream) in a cup to be analyzed.


    Remember: You should not urinate for at least 2 hours prior.

  • Should I take an oral or a rectal Trich test?

    Oral and rectal Trich tests are not recommended

  • How does a rapid Trich test work?

    Results from a rapid Trich test, which are 82-95% accurate, are available within 10 minutes.

  • How long does it take to get results?

    • Urine test: 3 business days
    • Swab test: 4 business days

  • What do my test results mean?

    There are three different scenarios for Trichomoniasis test (swab or urine) results:

    1. Positive Trichomoniasis test

    You have a Trichomoniasis infection that needs to be treated and your partner needs to be notified.

    2. Negative Trichomoniasis test

    This generally means you do not have Trichomonas.


    However, consider the following:

    • The timing of the test. Has enough time passed after a sexual encounter for the test to be most accurate? The ideal time for testing is 4 weeks after exposure
    • The overall accuracy of the particular test performed. The accuracy is 96% for the swab test versus 64-75% for the urine test

    3. Inconclusive or equivocal Trichomoniasis test

    Testing needs to be repeated:

    • The ideal test needs to be performed (swab)
    • The test needs to be performed after the ideal incubation period (4 weeks)
    • Both an alternative test and the test that had inconclusive results should be performed

  • Can I get treated for Trichomonas?

    Yes. This is called empiric treatment, which is initiated without a final diagnosis based on assumptions and clinical experience. There is consensus within medicine that empiric treatment can be provided to people:

    • Who experience signs and symptoms of Trichomoniasis (e.g., discharge from the genitals, painful and frequent urination, burning sensation during urination, or bleeding from the genitals after sexual intercourse or between menstrual periods)
    • Who had a sexual encounter with an infected person (even if there are no symptoms)
    • Who have a sexual partner who is positive for Trichomoniasis
    • Who are very anxious about this specific STD. It is best to not use antibiotics without an objective reason. However, if you are extremely worried about your STD status, you can get the needed medication from a healthcare provider

  • What treatments are available for Trichomonas?

    Three different treatment options are available:

    • One dose of Metronidazole (2 gm)
    • One dose of Tinidazole (2 gm)
    • Twice a day for seven days of Metronidazole (500 mg)

  • Where can I get tested for Trichomonas?

    Testing can be performed in primary care offices, urgent care clinics, OB/GYN clinics, Planned Parenthood clinics, free STD testing centers and our specialized STD center.

  • How do I protect my partner?

    For you to transmit Trich to your partner, your infected fluids have to come into contact with your partner’s mucosa. Do the following to prevent this from happening:

    • Avoid contact between your infected area and your partner’s skin while you have symptoms and for seven days after treatment
    • Don’t let your partner touch your infected area
    • Don’t share sex toys
    • Wash your hands with soap after touching your infected area

  • Should I tell my partner that I have Trichomoniasis?

    It is recommended that you notify all sexual partners within the last 60 days about your Trichomoniasis status and suggest they undergo a proper medical examination and testing. This will prevent further infection and possible health complications.


    If you are not comfortable with notifying your sexual partners, we can anonymously notify them on your behalf by calling, sending a text message or emailing.

  • Can I get reinfected with Trichomoniasis

    Yes. This is another reason for notifying your partner(s) to be tested and treated.

  • Is it necessary to get re-tested after treatment?

    The chance for Trichomoniasis reinfection is very high (17% get reinfected within 3 months) and retesting is recommended for women within 3 months of the initial treatment. Retesting can be performed as early as 2 weeks post-treatment.

  • Will my health insurance cover testing and treatment?

    Yes. Typically, health insurance covers the doctor’s office visit, testing and treatment.


    If you have a high deductible plan, we recommend that you call your insurance company and find out your contribution for the primary care office visit and T.vaginalis testing (ICD 73.88; CPT 87491 or 87591).

  • What is the cost of Trichomonas testing if I do not have health insurance?

    The cost of the office visit is $84 cash (or $90 credit) plus $60 for the testing that is performed in certified labs.
